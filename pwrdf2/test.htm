1. Cheryl Curtis, PWRDF's New Executive Director

The Primate's Fund staff were happy to welcome Cheryl Curtis in mid-September as Executive Director. An experienced leader and manager Cheryl draws on strong experience in organizational development, supervision and teambuilding in accepting this new challenge. A skilled facilitator and informed educator, Cheryl is committed to education for transformation and is a valuable resource to both our partners and our constituency in Canada.

As a faithful ecumenist and interfaith ally Cheryl brings a depth and breadth to our organizational relationships. Well rooted in both local and global aspects of ministry, she brings wisdom and experience to The Primate's Fund and The Anglican Church of Canada.

Cheryl comes to PWRDF from the Toronto Conference office of The United Church of Canada where she held the Social Justice and Ethic Ministries portfolio. She also brings her experience as the Executive Director of the Working Group on Refugee Resettlement in Toronto.

3. HIV/AIDS EVENTS

A Powerful Message About AIDS

This summer St. Thomas, Belleville in the Diocese of Ontario held a church service with AIDS as the theme. Youth led the service. To illustrate the impact of the AIDS pandemic on communities in Africa, two grandmothers and a grandfather from the congregation were given 38 dolls to represent the 38 grandchildren they were left to raise after the deaths of their own children. The message was clear: this cannot continue!

Thank you St. Thomas for this illustration of raising awareness and funding for the Partnership for Life initiative. How can this stimulate other ideas and action? How can other parishes integrate this illustration into a service?

9. Partnerships in Latin America

From our Latin America Desk - "It can take many years to become an overnight success." - In for the long haul with PWRDF partners in Latin America.

Many PWRDF partners know what it means to work for change over the long haul. For indigenous women's organizations in Latin America this is especially true as they face the challenges that come with being three-times marginalized: poor, indigenous and women. That they hang in for so many years, hoping for any small overnight success, is both a miracle and an inspiration to those of us who are privileged to accompany them.

In July of this year, PWRDF Latin America/Caribbean coordinator, Suzanne Rumsey visited partners in Guatemala and Mexico. In the tiny hamlet of Labor de Falla, she met with local members of the Women's Ecumenical Network, supported by the Conference of Evangelical Churches of Guatemala (CIEDEG). Dressed in their beautiful woven "trajes" (skirt and blouse), they quietly shared how they survived the genocide of Guatemala's 36-year civil war - and how many of their family members did not.

In her trip report, Suzanne notes: "It was out of this experience of extreme violence that in 1982 they began to organize themselves. Support from the Kaqchikel Presbytery (the Mayan Presbyterian church) followed. When I asked the women to describe what successes they have had over the years, the first one they described was overcoming their own fear and the resistance of their husbands to leave the house and become involved in an organized women's group. The issue of the lack of support, even resistance and denial of "permission" to participate, became a recurring theme in all of the meetings I had with Guatemalan women. Not for the first time it caused me to question the viability of women's development in a context where gender discrimination begins and remains entrenched at home."

But the fact that 23 years later these women continue to meet, to support one another, to organize and to participate in larger networks and coalitions is testimony to their quiet strength in the face of seemingly insurmountable odds. Suzanne goes on to note: "Through the Network the women have received workshops on a variety of themes; everything from family violence and women's self-esteem to free trade agreements and their impacts on local economies. Despite a lack of formal education, when describing free trade they were able to make the links between agreements negotiated by governments and how those agreements affect them. The women have also participated in marches and events that have brought together women from throughout Guatemala in relation to these themes. They described the sense of solidarity they experience; of being part of something larger, and of the importance of the friendships and mutual support they have gained both locally and nationally."

In Mexico, K'inal Antzetik is carrying out pioneering work in Mexican indigenous communities to address issues related to women's health and in particular, the high rates of maternal mortality. Suzanne wrote in her trip report that, "In recent years, K'inal's work has been centred in the states of Chiapas, Oaxaca and Guerrero, three states with a significant indigenous population and with many of the poorest municipalities in the country. Last year K'inal, working with governmental health agencies (virtually no NGOs operate in the region), established a "Casa de Salud" (Health Centre) for indigenous women in one of Guerrero's poorest municipalities - San Luís Acatlán. The Casa serves as an intermediary centre for women from remote communities whose pregnancies and deliveries have been determined to be high risk by health promoters linked to K'inal, and the municipality's hospital.

"K'inal has faced many challenges in establishing the Casa, including uneven government financing, resistance from husbands who do not wish their wives to leave their communities, and a lack of medical staff trained to work inter-culturally. But significant advances have been made and in May of this year, K'inal, together with the Centre for Analysis and Investigation (FUNDAR) convoked a forum on maternal health in Guerrero entitled "Prevention of Maternal Mortality in Mexico: And the Indigenous Women?". At its conclusion a formal agreement was signed between the participating NGOs, federal and state governmental agencies and the Mexican Congress for a comprehensive maternal health program for Guerrero, the first of its kind in the country."

An "overnight success" can indeed be many years in the making. But the persistence of indigenous women in Guatemala and NGOs like K'inal shows us that such success is possible.

10. Book Review - Betrayed: The Assassination of Digna Ochoa by Linda Diebel

Betrayed: The Assassination of Digna Ochoa - by Linda Diebel - The Murder of a High-Profile Human Rights Activist and the ensuing cover-up in the Mexican Government

Suzanne Rumsey, Development Program Coordinator, Latin American and the Caribbean, writes:

"During the decade that I worked on behalf of the Canadian churches in Mexico and Central America, a number of colleagues in the human rights community paid the ultimate price of speaking truth to power, among them, Digna Ochoa. Digna spoke truth to power and defended those at the margins of Mexican society. Her assassination, the first of a prominent human rights defender in Mexico in over a decade, and the first under the so-called "democracy" of the Fox administration, was a tremendous blow, both personally and professionally to her colleagues in the Mexican human rights community and to those of us in the international community who accompany our partners in Mexico. The impunity that this case still enjoys speaks truth to power about the continuing state of democracy in Mexico. As another Mexican colleague once put it: "We are only starting to learn how to someday become democratic".

In writing and publishing Digna's story, Linda Diebel continues to speak truth to power just as she did during her tenure in Mexico. From a 1994 report on human rights in Mexico, Inter-Church Committee on Human Rights in Latin America (ICCHRLA), a quote from an article written by Linda describes the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA) as "the death certificate for the indigenous people of Mexico". From the Toronto Star, April 1995 in which it is revealed that both the Mexican authorities and CSIS are investigating human rights defenders: "Mexico blacklists priest from Canada as rebel ally". And from the Toronto Star, April, 2001, reporting on a Canadian church leaders' delegation to northern Mexico: "Mexicans pay price for NAFTA".

Speaking truth to power can be difficult, at times costly. Human rights defenders and journalists know this. Digna knew this. Linda knows this. And those of us in the Canadian churches who collaborated with these two women over the years, owe them a very large debt of gratitude, as we too continue to seek ways to speak truth to power, with and on behalf of those at the margins of our societies and our economies."
